# Priority Voting

## What It Is
A democratic convergence technique where participants distribute a limited number of votes across options to surface collective priorities. Also known as "dot voting" or "multi-voting." The constraint of limited votes forces participants to make real trade-offs.

## When to Use
- Have many options (5+) and need to narrow down to the top few
- Group decision-making where everyone's voice should count
- Need a quick, low-conflict way to find consensus
- After brainstorming or affinity grouping, need to select what to pursue

## When NOT to Use
- Binary decision (use Pros & Cons instead)
- Decisions requiring deep analysis (voting captures preference, not reasoning)
- When one person has decision authority and just needs input (use Impact-Effort instead)

## Process Steps
1. **List all options** — Display all items clearly, numbered for reference.
2. **Set the rules**:
   - Each participant gets N votes (typically: total options ÷ 3, rounded up, minimum 3)
   - Can spread votes across different items or concentrate on fewer
   - Optionally: allow "super votes" (worth 2x) — each person gets 1 super vote
3. **Vote independently** — Each participant allocates their votes without seeing others' choices (reduces anchoring bias).
4. **Tally results** — Count votes per item. Rank from highest to lowest.
5. **Identify the winners** — Draw a clear line: top 3-5 items move forward.
6. **Discuss surprises** — Are there items with unexpected high/low votes? Briefly discuss why.
7. **Confirm or adjust** — Does the group agree with the result? If a critical item was missed, discuss whether to include it.

## Facilitator Prompts
- "We have [N] options. You each get [N÷3] votes. You can spread them or concentrate them."
- "Vote for the options you think are most important to pursue. Don't overthink it."
- "Here are the results. The top [3-5] are: [list]. Does this feel right?"
- "I notice [item] got zero votes even though we discussed it a lot. Why do you think that is?"
- "[Item] got the most votes by far — it seems like a clear priority. Any objections?"
- "There's a tie between [A] and [B]. Let's do a quick tiebreaker: if you could only pick one, which would it be?"
