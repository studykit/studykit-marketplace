# SWOT Analysis

## What It Is
A strategic framework that evaluates an idea, project, or situation across four dimensions: **S**trengths (internal positive), **W**eaknesses (internal negative), **O**pportunities (external positive), **T**hreats (external negative). Distinguishes between what you can control and what you can't.

## When to Use
- Evaluating a strategic decision or direction
- Comparing alternatives before choosing one
- Need to understand both internal capabilities and external landscape
- Problem-solving that requires strategic context

## When NOT to Use
- Early divergent brainstorming where judgment should be deferred
- Tactical, small-scale decisions that don't have strategic dimensions
- When the internal/external distinction isn't meaningful

## Process Steps
1. **Define the subject** — What are we analyzing? Be specific: a product, a strategy, a team, a decision.
2. **Strengths** (Internal, Positive) — What advantages do we have? What do we do well? What resources are available? What do others see as our strengths?
3. **Weaknesses** (Internal, Negative) — What could be improved? Where do we lack resources? What do we do poorly? What should we avoid?
4. **Opportunities** (External, Positive) — What trends can we exploit? What gaps exist in the market? What changes are happening that could benefit us?
5. **Threats** (External, Negative) — What obstacles do we face? What is the competition doing? Are there changing requirements or regulations? What external risks exist?
6. **Cross-analysis** — Generate strategies from combinations:
   - **S+O**: How can strengths capture opportunities?
   - **S+T**: How can strengths counter threats?
   - **W+O**: How can opportunities address weaknesses?
   - **W+T**: What weaknesses make threats most dangerous?

## Facilitator Prompts
- **Strengths**: "What's our unfair advantage here? What would others say we're great at?"
- **Weaknesses**: "Let's be honest — where are we lacking? What's our Achilles' heel?"
- **Opportunities**: "What's changing in the world that could work in our favor?"
- **Threats**: "What external forces could derail this, even if we execute perfectly?"
- **Cross**: "We're strong at [S] and there's an opportunity in [O] — how can we combine these?"
- **Cross**: "Our weakness in [W] makes us vulnerable to [T] — how do we mitigate this?"

## Example
**Subject**: Launching an open-source developer tool

| | Positive | Negative |
|---|----------|----------|
| **Internal** | **Strengths**: Strong engineering team, deep domain expertise, existing user base of 5K | **Weaknesses**: No community management experience, limited documentation, small marketing budget |
| **External** | **Opportunities**: Growing demand for dev tools in this space, no dominant OSS solution exists, conference season approaching | **Threats**: Large company could open-source competing tool, community expectations for responsiveness, licensing complexity |

**Cross-strategies**:
- **S+O**: Leverage domain expertise to become the go-to solution before a competitor enters
- **W+O**: Use conference season to recruit community contributors who can help with docs
- **S+T**: Engineering depth means we can iterate faster than a large company's side project
- **W+T**: Lack of community experience + high responsiveness expectations = hire or assign a dedicated community lead before launch
