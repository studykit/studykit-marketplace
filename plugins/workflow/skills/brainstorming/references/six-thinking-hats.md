# Six Thinking Hats

## What It Is
Edward de Bono's technique for structured parallel thinking. Each "hat" represents a different thinking mode. By wearing one hat at a time, the group focuses on one perspective before moving to the next, preventing chaotic arguments and ensuring all angles are covered.

## When to Use
- Need to analyze an idea or proposal from multiple perspectives
- Discussions tend to become adversarial or one-dimensional
- Want structured evaluation before making a decision
- Transitioning from divergent to convergent phase

## When NOT to Use
- Early divergent phase where free association is more valuable
- Simple decisions that don't warrant multi-perspective analysis
- Time is very limited — the full six-hat cycle takes sustained focus

## Process Steps
1. **White Hat (Facts)** — What do we know? What data do we have? What information is missing? Pure facts, no interpretation.
2. **Red Hat (Feelings)** — What's your gut reaction? First impressions, intuitions, emotions. No justification needed — just how it feels.
3. **Black Hat (Caution)** — What could go wrong? Risks, weaknesses, obstacles. Critical judgment — but based on logic, not emotion.
4. **Yellow Hat (Optimism)** — What's the best case? Benefits, value, opportunities. Why this could work — logical optimism.
5. **Green Hat (Creativity)** — What are the alternatives? New ideas, modifications, creative possibilities. This is the brainstorming hat.
6. **Blue Hat (Process)** — Step back: What have we learned? What's our conclusion? What are the next steps? Meta-thinking about the thinking.

Spend focused time on each hat. The key discipline is that everyone wears the same hat at the same time — no mixing perspectives within a phase.

## Facilitator Prompts
- **White**: "Let's start with just the facts. What do we know for certain? What data exists?"
- **Red**: "Forget the data for a moment. How does this make you feel? What's your gut saying?"
- **Black**: "Now let's be critical. What are the risks? What could fail? What are we not seeing?"
- **Yellow**: "Let's look at the bright side. What are the biggest potential benefits?"
- **Green**: "Creative time. How could we modify this? What alternatives exist? What's a completely different approach?"
- **Blue**: "Let's zoom out. What patterns emerged? What's our key takeaway? What do we do next?"

## Example
**Proposal**: Adopt a 4-day work week

- **White Hat**: Studies show 20% productivity increase in trials. Our industry average is 45-hour weeks. 3 competitors have tried it — 2 kept it, 1 reverted.
- **Red Hat**: Exciting but anxiety-inducing. Feels like a bold move. Worry about client expectations.
- **Black Hat**: Client-facing teams may struggle with coverage. Risk of work compression into fewer days causing burnout. May not suit all roles equally.
- **Yellow Hat**: Huge recruiting advantage. Reduced burnout long-term. Forces efficiency and meeting reduction. Demonstrates trust in employees.
- **Green Hat**: Hybrid model — 4-day weeks except during launch periods. Rotating day off for coverage. Trial with one team first. "Focus Fridays" as a stepping stone.
- **Blue Hat**: Strong enthusiasm with legitimate coverage concerns. Next step: pilot with one non-client-facing team for 3 months, measure productivity and satisfaction.
