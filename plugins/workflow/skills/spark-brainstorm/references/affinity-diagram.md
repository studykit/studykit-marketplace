# Affinity Diagram

## What It Is
A bottom-up grouping technique that organizes a large number of ideas, facts, or observations into natural clusters based on their relationships. Instead of imposing categories top-down, patterns emerge from the ideas themselves.

## When to Use
- Have a large collection of ungrouped ideas (10+) from a brainstorming session
- Need to find themes and patterns in diverse input
- Transitioning from divergent to structured thinking
- Making sense of qualitative data (user feedback, interview notes, feature requests)

## When NOT to Use
- Fewer than 8 ideas — grouping isn't needed yet
- Categories are already obvious and agreed upon
- Convergent phase — need to prioritize, not organize

## Process Steps
1. **List all items** — Write out every idea, fact, or observation as a separate item. One idea per item.
2. **Find natural pairs** — Look for two items that feel related. Place them together. Don't name the group yet.
3. **Build clusters** — Continue grouping related items together. Let the groups emerge naturally — don't force items into a category.
4. **Name each group** — Once clusters stabilize, give each group a short, descriptive header that captures its theme.
5. **Handle outliers** — Some items won't fit any group. That's fine — keep them as standalone items. They may represent unique insights.
6. **Identify super-groups** — If you have many clusters (7+), look for higher-level groupings. Create a hierarchy if helpful.
7. **Review and adjust** — Step back and look at the overall structure. Move items between groups if they fit better elsewhere. Split groups that feel too broad.

## Facilitator Prompts
- "Let me list all the ideas we've generated so far. Here they are: [list]"
- "Which of these feel like they belong together? Don't overthink it — go by instinct."
- "I see a cluster forming around [theme]. What should we call this group?"
- "This item could fit in [Group A] or [Group B]. Where does it feel more natural?"
- "[Item] doesn't fit anywhere yet — and that's okay. It might be a unique insight."
- "We have [N] groups now. Do any of these groups share a higher-level theme?"
- "Looking at the groups, which has the most items? That might be our biggest opportunity area."

## Example
**Raw ideas**: 20 ideas from a brainstorming session on "improving remote work"

**Resulting affinity groups**:

| Group | Items |
|-------|-------|
| **Communication** | Async video updates, better meeting notes, dedicated Slack channels, no-meeting Wednesdays |
| **Social Connection** | Virtual coffee chats, online game nights, annual in-person retreats, buddy system |
| **Workspace** | Home office stipend, ergonomic guide, noise-canceling headphones, co-working space credits |
| **Work-Life Balance** | Flexible hours, "right to disconnect" policy, mental health days, timezone-friendly scheduling |
| **Tooling** | Better video conferencing, shared whiteboard tool, project management upgrade, automated standup bot |
| **Outliers** | Four-day work week pilot, internal podcast series |
